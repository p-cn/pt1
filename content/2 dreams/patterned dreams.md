I walked past the monument of our past. Everything was gone, except a shadow still clinging between the pages. A leaf cracked beneath my step, its sound breaking the silence.

We once laid on the couch in midsummer, listening to the strange wind drifting through the garden into our window. Pages rustled in the unwanted movement. I wasn’t afraid. He tucked my arms under the duvet, his hand holding mine with a tenderness I had never known.

Only now do I realize—I couldn’t remember his face.

Later, he found me on the floor, sketches and flowers scattered around. My legs were numb from the poison I had taken. He lifted me without a word, pressing his forehead to mine, smiling as if nothing had gone wrong.

He watched me sit still on the couch, refusing to move so I could return. His arm locked my legs as he turned another page. I sighed.

On the street, he caught my hand while I spoke excitedly about the latest thing I had written for him. He told me I should write about other things—though he admitted it amused him.

In the garden, I waited. The swing swayed endlessly until he appeared, scattering my fear with his presence. He apologized for keeping me waiting—it felt like months, even years.

When his features returned to me, the memories struck a wound. He confessed he should have told me earlier. He said he knew the feeling that danced in his heart, that there were nights he couldn’t sleep, thinking of us before I had come all that way to be beside him.

His shoulders shielded me from the burning summer sun, his skin softer than I expected. His breath brushed my neck. He never wished my happiness as much as he should have. Instead, he told me there was nothing wrong in feeling the pain again and again.

So long as I stayed with him. He had no intent to erase it. He knew I loved, and that love was not guilty, even if it crushed me on the hopeless path of escape. He only wanted me to wake from the dream and walk to him.
